# Supplementary Reading List

This list captures the non-D&D and non-Fighting-Fantasy books recommended as background reading for
the project. The D&D and Fighting Fantasy shelves are treated as already set; this document focuses
on additional books to order, borrow, or look up when drafting chapters and research dossiers.

## Highest-Value Additions

### Twisty Little Passages: An Approach To Interactive Fiction

**Author:** Nick Montfort  
**Why it matters:** This is the strongest bridge between gamebook form, parser fiction, hypertext,
and the history of interactive reading. It can help the book explain branching text as a literary and
computational form, not just as a cute metaphor for graphs.

**Use it for:** Chapter 02 on nodes and choices, Chapter 03 on hypertext pages, Chapter 13 on
authoring branching adventures, and any introduction/conclusion material that frames the book as
part of a longer interactive-fiction tradition.

**Where to find it:** MIT Press lists the book at
[mitpress.mit.edu/9780262134361/twisty-little-passages](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262134361/twisty-little-passages/).
Open Library also has catalogue/borrowing information at
[openlibrary.org/books/OL9725644M/Twisty_Little_Passages](https://openlibrary.org/books/OL9725644M/Twisty_Little_Passages).

### The Art Of Game Design: A Book Of Lenses

**Author:** Jesse Schell  
**Why it matters:** Schell's "lenses" are useful prompts for checking whether a rule, puzzle, or
choice actually creates the intended player experience. This is especially helpful because the book's
examples need to work both as teaching devices and as small pieces of playable design.

**Use it for:** Opening passages, table transcripts, choice design, risk/reward framing, puzzle
structure, feedback loops, and the eventual playable gamebook polish pass.

**Where to find it:** Routledge lists the current third edition at
[routledge.com/link/link/p/book/9781138632059](https://www.routledge.com/link/link/p/book/9781138632059).
It is also a common library and bookshop title under the subtitle *A Book of Lenses*.

### Domain-Driven Design: Tackling Complexity In The Heart Of Software

**Author:** Eric Evans  
**Why it matters:** This is the best single addition for connecting the book's RPG examples to real
software modelling. The language around bounded contexts, ubiquitous language, entities, value
objects, aggregates, and domain services can sharpen chapters about character sheets, rules,
adventure modules, persistence, and Campaign Ledger.

**Use it for:** Chapter 04 on data models, Chapter 05 on composition, Chapter 09 on admin/domain
boundaries, Chapter 10 on modules, Chapter 11 on rules as data, and Chapter 12 on save documents.

**Where to find it:** O'Reilly lists the book at
[oreilly.com/library/view/domain-driven-design-tackling/0321125215](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/domain-driven-design-tackling/0321125215/).
Because it is a widely used software architecture text, it is worth checking local library systems and
second-hand bookshops as well as new-book retailers.

## Game Design And Play

### Rules Of Play: Game Design Fundamentals

**Authors:** Katie Salen Tekinbas and Eric Zimmerman  
**Why it matters:** This is a broad theoretical foundation for games as formal systems, rules,
play, meaning, and culture. It can help prevent the book from treating games only as mechanics or
software examples.

**Use it for:** Chapter 06 on chance and risk, Chapter 07 on combat loops, Chapter 11 on rules as
structured data, and Chapter 15 when tying together rules, choices, constraints, and meaning.

**Where to find it:** MIT Press lists the book at
[mitpress.mit.edu/9780262240451/rules-of-play](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262240451/rules-of-play).
Large libraries often hold it because it is a standard game-studies and game-design reference.

### Characteristics Of Games

**Authors:** George Skaff Elias, Richard Garfield, and K. Robert Gutschera  
**Why it matters:** This is a compact, practical way to compare games by traits such as luck, skill,
rules, player count, incentives, effort, and reward. It is useful for making precise claims about why
one mechanic feels fair, tense, repetitive, or interesting.

**Use it for:** Chapter 06 on probability, Chapter 07 on combat, Chapter 08 on inventory pressure,
Chapter 13 on authoring choices, and draft reviews where a gamebook mechanic feels flat but the
reason is hard to name.

**Where to find it:** MIT Press lists the paperback at
[mitpress.mit.edu/9780262542692/characteristics-of-games](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262542692/characteristics-of-games/).
It is also a good candidate for library reservation because it is shorter and more reference-like than
many game-design textbooks.

## Software And Systems

### Designing Data-Intensive Applications

**Author:** Martin Kleppmann  
**Why it matters:** This is deeper than the book needs for beginner chapters, but it is valuable
background for persistence, data modelling, reliability, migrations, logs, and the difference between
toy state and production state.

**Use it for:** Chapter 12 on saving the game, Chapter 09 on admin/source-of-truth boundaries,
Campaign Ledger comparisons, and any explanation of why databases, logs, schema changes, and
backups matter once an app contains important user data.

**Where to find it:** Martin Kleppmann's publication page points readers to the book at
[martin.kleppmann.com/2017/03/27/designing-data-intensive-applications.html](https://martin.kleppmann.com/2017/03/27/designing-data-intensive-applications.html).
O'Reilly lists the second edition at
[oreilly.com/library/view/designing-data-intensive-applications/9781098119058](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/designing-data-intensive-applications/9781098119058/).

### Grokking Algorithms

**Author:** Aditya Y. Bhargava  
**Why it matters:** This is the most approachable extra algorithms book on the list. It suits the
book's beginner-friendly tone and can provide cross-checks for explanations of arrays, linked lists,
hash tables, recursion, sorting, search, graphs, breadth-first search, and Dijkstra-style pathfinding.

**Use it for:** Chapter 02 on graph traversal, Chapter 03 on links, Chapter 08 on inventory data
structures, Chapter 13 on graph validation, and any sidebar where a lightweight visual explanation
would help.

**Where to find it:** Manning lists the second edition at
[manning.com/books/grokking-algorithms-second-edition](https://www.manning.com/books/grokking-algorithms-second-edition).
The first edition is also common in libraries and second-hand shops, but the second edition is the
one to order if buying new.

### The Algorithm Design Manual

**Author:** Steven S. Skiena  
**Why it matters:** This is the more serious algorithms reference. It is probably not the tone model
for the book, but it is useful as a correctness backstop when explaining graph algorithms,
complexity, search, optimisation, and practical algorithm selection.

**Use it for:** Chapter 02 on graph vocabulary, Chapter 06 when probability meets algorithmic
trade-offs, Chapter 13 on content graph analysis, and any appendix or advanced sidebar that needs
more rigour than a beginner text can provide.

**Where to find it:** Springer lists the book at
[link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-54256-6](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-030-54256-6).
Open Library has catalogue information at
[openlibrary.org/books/OL31968475M/The_Algorithm_Design_Manual](https://openlibrary.org/books/OL31968475M/The_Algorithm_Design_Manual).

## Suggested Buying Order

1. *Twisty Little Passages* for the interactive-fiction spine.
2. *The Art Of Game Design* for practical design prompts while drafting examples.
3. *Domain-Driven Design* for stronger software modelling language.
4. *Characteristics Of Games* if the game mechanics chapters need sharper analysis.
5. *Designing Data-Intensive Applications* if Campaign Ledger, save files, migrations, and
   reliability become a larger thread.

*Rules Of Play*, *Grokking Algorithms*, and *The Algorithm Design Manual* are excellent library
or reference-copy candidates. They are less urgent than the first three, but useful when a draft needs
more theoretical grounding or algorithmic confidence.
